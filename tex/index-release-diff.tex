\begin{abstract}
	The emergence of large-scale quantum computers makes migration of Internet of Things (IoT) systems to post-quantum cryptographic standards increasingly necessary. This paper studies the deployment of ML-DSA (Module-Lattice-Based Digital Signature Algorithm) in MQTT-based IoT systems on ARM Cortex-M4 microcontrollers from three connected perspectives: implementation optimization, MQTT-oriented signature integration, and system-level evaluation. An optimized ML-DSA implementation is developed by targeting the dominant arithmetic kernels on constrained devices, and a broker-compatible method is used to embed ML-DSA signatures into MQTT publish-subscribe communication. Experimental results indicate that ML-DSA still imposes substantial overhead relative to ECDSA, with signature generation latencies of \textbf{663--1,136\,ms} and computational overhead of \textbf{70.6--121.0$\times$} across parameter sets. End-to-end evaluation further quantifies message expansion, latency, throughput, and memory cost, providing practical guidance for ML-DSA deployment in resource-constrained MQTT environments.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\IEEEPARstart{T}{he} emergence of quantum computing undermines widely deployed public-key cryptographic primitives and makes migration to post-quantum standards a practical requirement rather than a long-term theoretical concern~\cite{Khalid2019}. This transition is especially important for Internet of Things (IoT) systems, where large numbers of distributed devices must operate for long periods under strict constraints on computation, memory, energy, and communication bandwidth.

\IEEEpubidadjcol

Among post-quantum primitives, ML-DSA (Module-Lattice-Based Digital Signature Algorithm) has been standardized by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) in FIPS 204~\cite{NIST-FIPS-204} as the primary digital signature scheme for post-quantum deployment. ML-DSA is particularly relevant in settings that require public verifiability, non-repudiation, auditability, and compatibility with certificate-based trust models. These properties, however, come with substantial implementation cost. ML-DSA signatures span 2,420--4,627 bytes across standardized parameter sets, which is roughly 30--70$\times$ larger than 64-byte ECDSA signatures~\cite{HwangKim2024}. In addition, the underlying lattice-based computations impose significantly higher processing overhead on low-power devices~\cite{Ghosh2019}.

These costs become more critical in MQTT-based IoT environments. MQTT is widely adopted because its publish-subscribe architecture and lightweight packet format suit resource-constrained sensing and control applications. The same design choices, however, also make the protocol sensitive to cryptographic expansion. When multi-kilobyte signatures are attached to short telemetry payloads, message size, transmission delay, and signing latency can dominate end-to-end performance. As a result, the feasibility of deploying standardized post-quantum signatures in practical MQTT workflows cannot be inferred from algorithm specifications alone and must be evaluated at the system level.

Existing studies have examined post-quantum signatures from several complementary perspectives. Embedded benchmarking studies have reported the cost of Dilithium and ML-DSA on ARM Cortex-M platforms~\cite{Banegas2021,pqm4-benchmarks}. MQTT-focused studies have shown that post-quantum authentication can substantially increase latency and payload size~\cite{Samandari2023,Rampazzo2023}, and some analyses have therefore emphasized KEM-based alternatives for constrained devices~\cite{Kim2025}. Nevertheless, a focused evaluation of standardized ML-DSA parameter sets within a complete MQTT publish-subscribe workflow on Cortex-M4-class microcontrollers remains limited. In particular, the relationship among cryptographic cost, message expansion, and protocol-level delay requires clearer empirical characterization.

This work addresses that gap through an empirical study of ML-DSA integration in MQTT-based IoT systems on ARM Cortex-M4 microcontrollers. The study is organized as a connected chain from implementation optimization to protocol integration and then to system-level evaluation. The main contributions are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Implementation-Level Optimization}: An optimized ML-DSA implementation is developed for ARM Cortex-M4 microcontrollers by targeting the dominant arithmetic kernels that determine execution cost in resource-constrained environments.

\item \textbf{MQTT-Oriented Signature Integration}: A broker-compatible integration method is designed to embed ML-DSA signatures into MQTT publish-subscribe communication while preserving practical deployability on constrained devices.

\item \textbf{System-Level Evaluation and Deployment Analysis}: Cycle-accurate and end-to-end experiments quantify computational overhead, message expansion, latency, throughput, and memory cost across all three standardized ML-DSA parameter sets relative to an ECDSA P-256 baseline, providing guidance for constrained IoT deployment.
\end{enumerate}

Section~\ref{sec:architecture} describes the \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{system architecture}\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{optimized implementation and MQTT-oriented integration method}\DIFaddend . Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} presents experimental evaluation. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes with implications for deployment.

\DIFaddbegin \subsection{\DIFadd{Implementation-Level Optimization}}

\DIFadd{The implementation-level optimization targets the arithmetic kernels that dominate ML-DSA execution time on Cortex-M4 microcontrollers. As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:complexity}, these kernels are concentrated in NTT-based polynomial multiplication, coefficient-wise modular operations, and the surrounding reduction steps. Rather than changing the ML-DSA algorithm or parameter sets, the implementation focuses on reducing the cost of these dominant operations while preserving compatibility with the standardized design in FIPS 204~\mbox{%DIFAUXCMD
\cite{NIST-FIPS-204}}\hskip0pt%DIFAUXCMD
.
}

\DIFadd{Three optimization directions are applied. First, critical arithmetic routines are mapped to hand-tuned ARM Cortex-M4 instructions in order to improve instruction scheduling and reduce unnecessary memory traffic in repeated polynomial operations. Second, modular reduction is organized around Montgomery-domain computation so that expensive division-like operations are avoided in the main arithmetic path. Third, reduction is deferred when intermediate value ranges permit it, which lowers the number of normalization steps inside NTT butterfly sequences. Together, these changes target the implementation bottlenecks that are most relevant in resource-constrained devices.
}

\DIFadd{The optimized implementation retains the standard signing interface required by the communication layer. Key generation is invoked through }\texttt{\DIFadd{crypto\_sign\_keypair()}}\DIFadd{, signatures are generated through }\texttt{\DIFadd{crypto\_sign()}}\DIFadd{, and validation is performed through }\texttt{\DIFadd{crypto\_sign\_verify()}}\DIFadd{. This interface preserves a clean boundary between cryptographic processing and MQTT message handling, which is important for the system-level evaluation in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}.
}

\DIFdelend \section{Experimental Evaluation}\label{sec:evaluation}

\subsection{Methodology}\label{sec:methodology}

\subsubsection{Measurement Framework}

Performance profiling uses the ARM Cortex-M4 Data Watchpoint and Trace (DWT) unit for cycle-accurate measurement through the \texttt{DWT\_CYCCNT} register. This hardware-based approach avoids software profiling overhead and provides single-cycle temporal resolution. Measurement accuracy was validated by comparison with external logic-analyzer traces, confirming $\pm$1 cycle precision.

Memory-utilization analysis combines static and dynamic measurement techniques. Static memory consumption is quantified with the \texttt{arm-none-eabi-size} toolchain utilities. Dynamic memory profiling uses stack watermarking with 0xDEADBEEF sentinel values at 32-byte intervals. Energy consumption is assessed with INA219 current sensor modules that measure supply current at a sampling frequency of 100\,Hz.

\subsubsection{Software Configuration}

The software environment uses ARM GCC toolchain version 10.3.1 with \texttt{-O3} optimization. Preliminary testing showed that \texttt{-O3} improved performance by 18--23\% over \texttt{-O2}, at the cost of a 12--15\% increase in code size. ML-DSA implementations use the pqm4 reference library with verified NIST test vector compliance. ECDSA baseline measurements use the micro-ecc library with NIST P-256 curves.

MQTT protocol integration uses the Eclipse Paho MQTT Embedded C client communicating with a Mosquitto MQTT broker (version 2.0.15). The network infrastructure uses IEEE 802.11n in the 2.4-GHz band with controlled signal strength from -45 to -52 dBm and packet loss below 0.1\%.

\subsubsection{Benchmark Design}

Each parameter set is evaluated for representative IoT message payloads of 10 bytes (single-sensor readings, 23\% of observed traffic), 50 bytes (multi-parameter telemetry, 51\%), and 100 bytes (diagnostic reports, 18\%). Statistical reliability is supported by 1,000 repeated measurements with interquartile-range-based outlier elimination, which yields rejection rates of 0.8--2.3\%. All measurements are conducted under temperature-controlled conditions (25$^\circ$C $\pm$2$^\circ$C) with device voltage stabilized at 3.3 V $\pm$1\%.

\subsubsection{Deployment Feasibility}

Deployment feasibility is evaluated across representative IoT application categories based on the measured performance characteristics.

\textbf{High-throughput sensor networks}: Frequent authenticated message publication creates a computational bottleneck that limits sustainable publication rates to 0.87--1.52 messages/s across ML-DSA parameter sets, compared with 104--109 messages/s for ECDSA. Deployments requiring publication rates above 1 message/s therefore need mitigation strategies such as message aggregation, publisher-side caching of unchanged states, or hybrid authentication in which ML-DSA is reserved for security-critical messages and MAC-based authentication is used for routine telemetry. For example, a network with 100 sensor nodes, each publishing at 0.01 Hz, yields an aggregate throughput of 1 message/s and remains within the computational capacity of ML-DSA-44.

\textbf{Battery-powered devices}: Energy-constrained devices require additional consideration of signing cost. A signature-generation cost of 110.4 million cycles at 168 MHz corresponds to 657 ms for ML-DSA-44 and implies power draw of approximately 50 mW, assuming a 3.3-V supply and 15-mA active current. The resulting energy per signature is 32.9 mJ. For a device that transmits 100 authenticated messages per day from a 2,000-mAh battery (3.3-V nominal voltage, 23.8 kJ total energy), ML-DSA signature generation consumes 3.29 J per day, or 13.8\% of the total energy budget. This estimate excludes network-transmission energy and verification overhead at subscriber devices. ML-DSA therefore remains viable for battery-powered publishers with low daily message frequencies, whereas higher publication rates require energy harvesting or mains power.

\textbf{Real-time control systems}: Systems with sub-second latency requirements violate their timing constraints under ML-DSA end-to-end authentication latency of 1.11--1.88 s. Deployments requiring real-time authenticated command-response interactions therefore need architectural accommodations such as asymmetric publisher-subscriber security models, pre-signed command templates for restricted command spaces, or hybrid cryptographic modes that use classical signatures for time-critical operations and periodic ML-DSA re-authentication for long-term security. Pure ML-DSA authentication remains suitable for monitoring applications that tolerate multi-second latency, but it is not appropriate for closed-loop control systems requiring deterministic sub-second response.

\subsubsection{Optimization Opportunities}

Several optimization strategies can mitigate ML-DSA performance overhead in resource-constrained deployments.

\textbf{Parameter set selection}: Deployment-specific security analysis may support the use of ML-DSA-44, which reduces signing latency by 41.6\% (1,136\,ms $\rightarrow$ 663\,ms), signature size by 47.7\% (4,627 bytes $\rightarrow$ 2,420 bytes), and memory consumption by 47.3\% (43.1\,KB $\rightarrow$ 22.7\,KB) relative to ML-DSA-87 while maintaining NIST security level 2 protection.

\textbf{Message aggregation}: Batch signature generation over aggregated sensor readings amortizes cryptographic overhead across multiple measurements. Aggregating $N$ measurements into a single signed payload reduces the per-measurement signing overhead from 657\,ms to $657/N$\,ms, at the cost of an $N\times$ increase in sampling-interval latency.

\textbf{Hybrid authentication}: Selective ML-DSA deployment for security-critical messages combined with MAC-based authentication for routine telemetry reduces average authentication overhead while preserving non-repudiation for critical operations. For a traffic mix of 90\% routine telemetry (MAC: 0.5\,ms) and 10\% critical messages (ML-DSA-44: 657\,ms), the weighted average overhead is 66.2\,ms, compared with 657\,ms for pure ML-DSA deployment.

\textbf{Hardware acceleration}: Future ARM Cortex-M processors with cryptographic acceleration support for NTT operations could provide 5--10$\times$ performance improvements and reduce ML-DSA signing latency to below 100 ms.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presents a performance characterization of ML-DSA integration in MQTT-based IoT systems on ARM Cortex-M4 microcontrollers. Signature-generation latencies of 663--1,136\,ms correspond to 70.6--121.0$\times$ overhead relative to ECDSA P-256 and identify signing as the primary computational bottleneck, limiting sustainable message throughput to 0.87--1.52 messages/s. Memory requirements of 22.7--43.1\,KB SRAM and 37.2--73.9\,KB Flash further constrain deployment to mid-range microcontrollers.

ML-DSA remains viable for applications that tolerate multi-second latency and sub-hertz publication frequencies, including environmental monitoring, asset tracking, and periodic telemetry reporting. Real-time control systems that require sub-second response need architectural accommodations or hybrid authentication approaches. Future work includes evaluating hardware acceleration on emerging ARM Cortex-M processors with cryptographic extensions and studying batch-signature-verification schemes for gateway devices.
